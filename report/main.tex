% Medical image fusion aims to combine multiple medical images of the same body region using different imaging modalities, such as Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), Positron Emission Tomography (PET), Single-Photon Emission Computed Tomography (SPECT), or Computed Tomography (CT) into a single, more informative image. The goal is to assist doctors in diagnosing and evaluating medical problems more accurately.  Although each imaging modality offers unique advantages, none can perfectly capture all the necessary diagnostic information on its own, which can affect the accuracy of clinical decisions. Medical image fusion addresses this limitation by combining images from multi modalities to enhance diagnostic clarity. Numerous methods have been proposed in recent years, including deep learning, traditional image processing techniques—with or without optimization—or a combination of all three. However, current fusion methods often struggle with several limitations: poor-quality input images, loss of important structural details, and the inability to preserve fine anatomical features such as sulcal and gyral structures in brain images. These issues can significantly reduce the diagnostic value of the fused results. In this thesis, we propose a method centered on three techniques: deep learning, image processing, and optimization. Firstly, the input images are passed through the YCbCr color space combined with interpolation-based normalization. Secondly, Bilateral Texture Filtering (BTF) is utilized to decompose input images into texture and detail layers. Thirdly, a pre-trained ResNet-101 model using transfer learning is employed to extract features from the detail layers. Finally, the Dynamic Arithmetic Optimization Algorithm (DAOA) is applied to fuse the texture layers. Our method achieves a competitive result in all three image quality indices metrics with 0.31, 0.35 and 0.09 across the average quality index $Q_{ALI}$, contrast index $Q_{CI}$ and average gradient $Q_{AG}$. Additionally, a 0.90 score in weighted fusion quality $Q_W$ and 0.81 score in edge-dependent fusion quality index $Q_E$ shows the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

% Zubair et al. \cite{zubair} and Diwakar et al. \cite{diwakar} present an overview of multi-modality based medical image fusion techniques, including algorithms, methodologies, and the challenges involved. From these foundations, multi-modal medical image fusion has three main categories: image processing, deep learning and hybrid approach \cite{dinh1}. The image processing category itself is commonly divided into two branches: spatial domain and transform domain methods.  
% \\
% \\
% Spatial-domain fusion combines multiple source images by directly manipulating their individual pixel intensities.  This method directly processes pixel intensities in the spatial domain, without converting the image to a different representation. Common spatial-domain techniques include weighted averaging, spatial filtering, maximum or minimum pixel selection, and advanced methods such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and Intensity-Hue-Saturation (IHS) \cite{zubair}. For example, He et al.  \cite{ihspca} used the IHS transform to separate the multispectral image into intensity, hue, and saturation components. PCA was then applied to the PET image to extract principal components, and spatial frequency was used to weight them. The fused intensity was combined with the original hue and saturation to generate the final image. Although this approach preserves spatial structure and location, the fused image may lose fine details and edges, as well as showing low contrast and limited feature enhancement. 
% \\
% \\
% To overcome those challenges, a transform-domain fusion is proposed. Transform-domain fusion decomposes the input images into their frequency or spectral representations, using fusion methods to fuse the components in the corresponding frequency, and reconstruct the fused components to produce the output fused image. There are many popular transformation method such as Wavelet Transform, Laplacian Pyramid Transform, Contourlet Transform and Shearlet Transform \cite{f8}. Despite its improvements from the spatial-domain, its performance heavily depends on parameter selection, and the multi-level decomposition and reconstruction process can result in the loss of fine details, particularly in high-frequency regions \cite{dinh1}. This limitation can be seen in the work of Wang et al. \cite{lpt}, where the Laplacian Pyramid was implemented for image fusion.

Meanwhile, pre-trained models not only require less data but also consistently outperform models trained from scratch, as they transfer features learned from large-scale datasets.

For example, Dinh \cite{dinh1} implemented a pre-trained ResNet-101 model to extract features and fuse the high-frequency layer, while Do et al. \cite{f8} used a similar method using VGG-19. Unlike the first method, the second approach uses deep learning end-to-end to fuse the input images directly. Recent studies have shown various architectures for this purpose, including Transformer-based models \cite{trans}, generative adversarial networks (GAN) \cite{gan}, convolutional neural networks and attention-based framework \cite{cnnatt}. For example, Guo et al. \cite{cnnatt} proposed a novel medical image fusion framework called VANet that combines convolutional neural networks with an attention mechanism. This approach aims to enhance the quality  of multimodal medical image fusion by fusing both global contextual information and local structural details into a single framework.

Using the Weighted Mean Curvature Filter (WMCF) to decompose, CoatiOA guides low-frequency fusion to maintain brightness and contrast.

% \subsection{Bilateral Texture Filtering (BTF)}
% BTF is a structure-preserving image decomposition operator designed to seperate fine-scale texture details from the original image. Proposed by Cho et al \cite{btf}, While BTF inherits the advantages of the original bilateral filter—such as simplicity, locality, ease of implementation, scalability, and adaptability to various application scenarios—it improves upon the original by more effectively removing texture while preserving main image structures. However, this improvement comes with the downside of an increased computational complexity. The key idea to extract local feature without obscuring structure is patch shift. 
% \\
% \\
% Patch shift works by selecting, for each pixel, texture information from a neighboring patch that ignores prominent structural edges and best represents the texture region surrounding the pixel. This process effectively performs a soft segmentation of texture areas while preserving important structures in the image.
% Therefore, the only modification to the traditional bilateral filter is the computation of guidance image via patch shift. 
% \\
% \\
% Given a scalar-valued input image $I$, the new texture-filtering bilateral filter computes an output image J by this equation:
% \begin{equation}
% \label{eq:joint}
% J_p = \frac{1}{k_p} \sum_{q \in \Omega_p} f(\|q - p\|) g(\|G_q - G_p\|) I_q
% \end{equation}
% \\
% \\
% Where: 
% \begin{itemize}
%     \item $k_p$ is a normalizing term. 
%     \item $\Omega_p$ is a spatial neighborhood centered around the point $p$.
%     \item $X_p$ is computed as the weighted average of its neighboring values within $\Omega_p$, as defined by the BTF. 
%     \item $f$ is the spatial kernel, $g$ is the range kernel, both are modeled as Gaussian functions. 
%     \item $G$ is the texture description image, also known as guidance image in the context of joint bilateral filtering \cite{jbt}
% \end{itemize}
% \\
% \\
% $G_p$ can be computed by using local image statistics in a rectangular patch $\Omega_p$ centered at $p$. However, the problem is when this patch includes both texture and structural elements, these local statistics might obscure the presence of salient edges or distinct region boundaries. For example, two neighboring patches - each of size $k \times k$ - centered at adjacent pixels $p = (i, j)$ and $q = (i, j+1)$ must have a large overlap of size $k \times (k-1)$. As a result, the local statistics for $p$ and $q$ should be equaled, even when those two points are on opposite sides of an edge. 
% \\
% \\
% Patch shift overcomes this problem by assuming a $k \times k$ box as a patch, each pixel $p$ has a total of $k^2$ patches in image $I$ that contains $p$. In these $k^2$ patches, the patch $\Omega_p$ that is least likely to contain a prominent structural edge is selected. Furthermore, $B_q$ can be computed by using the average intensity within that patch $\Omega_q$, as the representative texture value $G_p$ at pixel $p$ in equation \ref{eq:joint}. 
% \\
% \\
% Although there are many choices for defining a texture measure to ensures seperation from structure edges, Cho et al. define texture as fine-scale spatial oscillations as below:
% \begin{equation}
% \Delta(\Omega_q) = I_{\max}(\Omega_q) - I_{\min}(\Omega_q)
% \label{eq:tonal_range}
% \end{equation}

% where $I_{\max}(\Omega_q)$ and $I_{\min}(\Omega_q)$ are the maximum and minimum image intensities in $\Omega_q$, respectively. After that, patch shift chooses the patch with the lowest tonal range to minimize the probability of involving a salient edge during texture feature computation.
% \\
% \\
% For every $p$ in the input image $I$, the average intensity $B_p$ is precalculated within its own center patch $\Omega_p$. And then, $G_p$ at $p$ is assigned the value of $B_q$, at $q$ that has the smallest $\Delta(\Omega_p)$ in the neighborhood of $p$. 
% \\
% \\
% Given an input image $I$, The filtering process can be computed by the following steps:
% \begin{itemize}
%     \item Apply a $k \times k$ box kernel to calculate the average image $B$.
%     \item For each pixel $p$, compute the tonal range $\Delta(\Omega_q)$.
%     \item Use patch shift on each pixel to obtain the guidance image $G$:
%     \begin{itemize}
%         \item For each pixel $p$, find the patch $\Omega_{q}$ whose $\Delta(\Omega_q)$ is the minimum among $k^2$ candidates.
%         \item Set $G_p = B_{q}$ or $B_{p}$
%     \end{itemize}
%     \item Apply the joint bilateral filter on the input image $I$, using the guidance image $G$, to obtain the output image $J$.
% \end{itemize}
% \\
% \\
% Although this process is generally effective for texture-structure decomposition, two modifications are implemented to improve its robustness.
% \\
% \\
% First of all, based on Equation \ref{eq:tonal_range}, the patch shift may not work efficiently if the tonal range within a pure texture region is as large as, or even larger than, the nearby structure edge. However, this problem is solved by re-defining Relative Total Variation (RTV), modifying the original version proposed by Xu et al. \cite{xu2011}, and refer to it as modified Relative Total Variation:

% \begin{equation}
% \label{eq:mrtv}
% \text{mRTV}(\Omega_q) = \Delta(\Omega_q) \frac{\max\limits_{r \in \Omega_q} |\partial_r|}{\sum\limits_{r \in \Omega_q} |\partial_r| + \epsilon}
% \end{equation}

% where $|\partial_r|$ is the gradient magnitude at pixel $r \in \Omega q$ and $\epsilon$ is a small number to avoid division by zero. Cho et al. set $|\partial_r|$ = $\sqrt{(\partial_x I_r)^2_r + (\partial_y I_r)^2_r})$ and $\epsilon$ = $10^{-9}$ in their research. The tonal range $\Delta(\Omega_q)$ functions as a scaling factor in Equation \ref{eq:mrtv}, capturing the absolute magnitude of the signal. 
% \\
% \\
% The mRTV value is relatively high in a structure patch that contain only a few edges, and low in texture patch with frequent oscillations. For a $k \times k$ 2D patch, $\frac{mRTV(\Omega_q)}{\Delta(\Omega_q)}$ would be around $\frac{1}{k}$ for a horizontal or vertical step edge, and $\frac{1}{k^2}$ for a texture patch with full oscillations. For that reason, mRTV can be used for texture filtering with arbitrarily large magnitudes. The modified patch shift operation can locate a pure texture patch among $k^2$ by selecting the patch $\Omega_q$ with the lowest mRTV value.
% \\
% \\
% Second of all, the mRTV values are very small in a smooth or flat areas of an image, which can increase their sensitivity to noise. To avoid that, when $G_p$ is set equal to $B_q$, two mRTV values of $(\Omega_p$ and $(\Omega_q$ are compared. If $(\Omega_p)$ and $(\Omega_q)$ are local statistics similar, $B_p$ is chosen as the value of $G_p$. However, if and only if $mRTV(\Omega_q)$ is smaller than $mRTV(\Omega_p)$, then $B_q$ is used for $G_p$. This method can be implemented by interpolating images $B$ and $G$ using the difference in $mRTV$ values as shown in the formula below:

% \begin{equation}
% G'_p = \alpha_p G_p + (1 - \alpha_p) B_p
% \end{equation}
% \\
% \\
% where
% \begin{equation}
% \label{alphap}
%     \alpha_p = 2 \left( \frac{1}{1 + \exp(-\sigma_\alpha (\mathrm{mRTV}(\Omega_p) - \mathrm{mRTV}(\Omega_q)))} - 0.5 \right)
% \end{equation}
% \\
% \\
% The weight $\alpha_p \in [0, 1]$ is small inside smooth or texture regions, but large around edges. Equation \ref{alphap} uses $\alpha_p$ to control the sharpness of the weight transition from edges to smooth or texture regions, increasing $\alpha_p$ leads to a sharper transition. $\alpha_p$ is set to $5k$ via the authors.

% For image denoising, a single iteration of bilateral filtering is often effective. However, texture may have larger patterns - in spatial and/or range scales - compared to that noise. % \subsubsection{Piella Metrics}
% Piella metrics evaluates the structural similarity between the input and fused images. Before explaining about the Piella metrics, we first provide a brief introduction to the Wang-Bovik image quality index, which serves as a foundational component in the computation of Piella's structural similarity evaluation.
% \\
% \\
% Given two input images $A$ and $B$, we first compute the mean values $\bar{A}$ and $\bar{B}$, the variances $\sigma_A^2$ and $\sigma_B^2$, and the covariance $\sigma_{AB}$. we compute the $Q_0$ as follows:
% \begin{equation}
% Q_0 = \frac{\sigma_{AB}}{\sigma_{A}\sigma_{B}} \times \frac{2\bar{A}\bar{B}}{\bar{A}^2 + \bar{B}^2} \times \frac{2\sigma_A\sigma_B}{\sigma^2_A + \sigma^2_B}
% \end{equation}

% The value $Q_O$ = $Q_0{(A, B)}$ is a measure for the similarity of the vectors $A$ and $B$ and takes values in the range [-1, 1]. 
% \\
% \\
% A sliding window approach is also implemented. Starting from the top-left corner of the input images, a fixed-size window with $n$ pixels moves across the images one pixel at a time until the bottom-right corner is reached. For each window $w$, the local quality index $Q_0(A, B \mid w)$ using the value $A(i, j)$ and $B(i, j)$ , where  pixels $(i, j)$  are in the sliding window $w$. Finally, the overall image quality index $Q_0$ is calculated as the formula below:
% \begin{equation}
%     Q_0(A, B) = \frac{1}{|W|} \sum_{w \in W} Q_0(A, B \mid w),
% \end{equation}

% where $W$ is the family of all windows and $|W|$ is the cardinality of $W$ \cite{piella}
% \\
% \\
% In Piella metric, $Q_0$ in the Wang-Bovik image quality index is used to define a quality index $Q(A, B, F)$. A saliency of image A in window $w$, denoted $s(a \mid w)$, is used to illustrate the local relevance of image A within $w$. Given $s(a \mid w)$ and $s(b \mid w)$, the local weight $\lambda(\omega)$ measuring the relative importance between two input images, is calculated as follow:

% \begin{equation}
% \lambda(w) = \frac{s(a|w)}{\sigma(a|w) + s(b|w)}.
% \end{equation}
% \\
% \\
% Now we define the fusion quality index \( Q(a, b, f) \) as

% \begin{equation}
% Q(a, b, f) = \frac{1}{|W|} \sum_{w \in W} \left[ \lambda(w) Q_0(a, f|w) + (1 - \lambda(w)) Q_0(b, f|w) \right].
% \end{equation}
% \\
% \\
% As a result, in areas where image $A$ is much more salient than image $B$, the quality index is primarily determined by image $A$. in contrast, in regions where image $B$ is much larger than $A$, the index is mostly determined by image $B$. However,  all local windows are treated equally in this evaluation. This approach doesn't align with the human visual system (HVS), as it is focusing more on visualize salient areas. To better match human perception, Piella et al. defined another variant of the fusion quality index that adds more weight to windows. The overall saliency of a window is defined as $C(w) = max(s(a \mid  w), s(b \mid w)$. The weighted fusion quality index $Q_W$ is then defined as

% \begin{equation}
% Q_W(A, B, F) = \sum_{w \in W} c(w) \left[ \lambda(w) Q_0(A, F|w) + (1 - \lambda(w)) Q_0(B, F|w) \right],
% \end{equation}

% \\
% \\
% where \( c(w) = C(w) / \left( \sum_{w' \in W} C(w') \right) \). 
% \\
% \\
% Finally, Piella et al. modified the fusion quality index to take some aspect of the HVS. Instead of using the original grayscale images $A, B$ and $F$,  $W$ can be evaluates by using edge images. Let $A'$ represent the edge image corresponding to $A$. The standard index $Q_{w}(A, B, F)$ is then combined with the edge-based index $Q_{W}(A', B', F')$ to produce an edge-dependent fusion quality index as follows:
% \begin{equation}

% \section{Conclusion and Perspective}
% \label{sec:conclusion}
% In this thesis, we have successfully developed a medical image fusion method designed to fuse structural and functional information from multiple imaging modalities, which combines the DAOA algorithm with a ResNet-101 model. The input images are first decomposed into texture and detail layers by using the Bilateral Texure Filtering. A fusion method for detail layers is designed based on the ResNet-101 model to prevents the lost of details in the output image. The texture layers are fused by a fusion method that is built based on the DAOA algorithm. The method ensures the quality of the resulting image in terms of contrast and brightness. The proposed approach shows strong performance in preserving fine details while enhancing functional contrast, achieving promising results across multiple datasets and evaluation metrics. Moreover, the method effectively overcomes several mentioned challenges in the field, including lack of contrast and detail preservation. We implemented six quantitative metrics for measuring the quality of the fused images.
% The experimental results showed that the proposed method has a competitive results with the existing fusion techniques across all six evaluation metrics.
% \\
% \\
% Despite its effectiveness, the proposed method still faces several challenges for further improvement. One key issue is the over-enhancement in PET regions, which can lead to unnatural brightness and potentially mislead clinical interpretation. Another problem is the suboptimal preservation of edge information, as illustrated in the lower $Q_{AB/F}$ scores. This shows that fine structural transitions and boundary details are not being preserved effectively, which may decrease the diagnostic quality of the fused images in anatomically sensitive areas. Future work will focus on four main directions aimed at improving both the quality and applicability of the proposed fusion method. Firstly, we aim to address the current limitations of our approach, particularly the over-enhancement observed in PET regions and the suboptimal edge preservation. For this challenge, we will explore both deep learning and advanced image processing techniques to improve edge preservation from the input images. Secondly, we plan to replace the ResNet-101 model with a more suitable pre-trained architecture that can better preserve fine details from the input images, thereby enhancing the overall quality of the fusion output. Thirdly, we intend to redesign the fitness function used in the fusion of texture layers. The current function lacks clarity in its formulation and may affect the contrast and the brightness quality of the fused images, which may cause the over-bright problem as mentioned above. To address this limitation, we plan to do a comprehensive review of existing fitness functions and develop a more effective formulation tailored to our fusion objectives. Finally, while our experiments focused on MRI and PET modalities, the proposed fusion method has the potential to be extended to other medical imaging types such as SPECT, CT, X-ray, and Ultrasound. Exploring its adaptability across a wider range of modalities will be an important direction for future research.
